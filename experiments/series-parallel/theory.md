# Series & Parallel Circuits

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** Electronic components can be wired together in two fundamental ways: Series (daisy-chained one after another) or Parallel (creating separate independent branches). How a circuit is wired completely changes how current and voltage are distributed across the components.
**Real-World Example:** Old Christmas tree lights were wired in **Series**. If one single bulb burned out and broke the chain, the entire string of lights would go dark! Modern home wiring uses **Parallel** circuits. That's why you can turn off the light switch in your bedroom, but the TV in the living room stays fully powered on.

## Formulas Used
**For Series Circuits:** 
Current only has one path. It must push through every single resistor. Therefore, resistance aggregates and current is uniform everywhere.
*   $R_{\text{total}} = R_1 + R_2 + ... + R_n$
*   $I_{\text{total}} = I_1 = I_2 = ... = I_n$
*   $V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 + ... + V_n$

**For Parallel Circuits:**
Current has multiple choices of paths, splitting like a river. Therefore, total resistance drops as you add more paths, and voltage is identical across every branch.
*   $\frac{1}{R_{\text{total}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + ... + \frac{1}{R_n}$
*   $I_{\text{total}} = I_1 + I_2 + ... + I_n$
*   $V_{\text{total}} = V_1 = V_2 = ... = V_n$

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **How do series and parallel setups affect voltage and current?** to observe firsthand why adding resistors in parallel actually *increases* the total current leaving the battery!

## Simulation Controls
*   **Mode Toggle:** Switch between a pure Series layout and a pure Parallel layout. 
*   **Voltage ($V$):** Adjust the source battery voltage.
*   **Resistors ($R_1$ & $R_2$):** Independently adjust the two light bulbs. 
*   **Visual Overlays:**
    *   **Electron Flow:** Notice how in series, electrons move steadily through the entire loop. In parallel, watch the main current split at the junction. If one resistor is set high and the other low, watch the electrons choose the "path of least resistance"!
