# Photoelectric Effect

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** The Photoelectric Effect is the phenomenon where a material (usually a metal) emits electrons when light is shone upon it. This groundbreaking discovery proved that light behaves not just like a wave, but like individual packets of energy called "photons."
**Real-World Example:** Solar panels! When sunlight strikes the silicon cells on a roof, the incoming photons knock electrons loose from the silicon atoms. These free electrons are then corralled into a circuit, creating the electric current that powers your home. 

## Formulas Used
Albert Einstein won the Nobel Prize for mathematically describing this using the photon energy equation:

**Energy of a Single Photon ($E_{\text{photon}}$):**
$$
E_{\text{photon}} = h \cdot f = \frac{hc}{\lambda}
$$
Where:
*   $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$)
*   $f$ is the frequency of the light.
*   $\lambda$ is the wavelength of the light (usually measured in nanometers, nm). Notice energy is inversely proportional to wavelength! (Shorter wave = higher energy).

**Kinetic Energy of Emitted Electron ($KE_{\text{max}}$):**
For an electron to escape, the photon hitting it must have more energy than the "Work Function" ($\Phi$), which is the intrinsic gripping force of the metal. If it does, the leftover energy becomes the electron's kinetic (movement) energy:
$$
KE_{\text{max}} = E_{\text{photon}} - \Phi
$$
If $E_{\text{photon}} \le \Phi$, absolutely zero electrons are emitted, no matter how bright the light is!

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **How does light frequency eject electrons from metal?** to understand how raising intensity (volume of photons) does nothing if the frequency (energy per photon) isn't high enough to overcome the metal's grip.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Wavelength (nm):** Adjust the color/frequency of the laser. Moving this into the ultraviolet range (shorter wavelengths) drastically increases the photon impact energy.
*   **Intensity:** Adjusts how many photons are fired per second (brightness). If the wavelength is wrong, turning up intensity does nothing! But if the wavelength is right, more intensity = more electrons emitted.
*   **Work Function ($\Phi$):** This represents changing the target metal plate. A higher value means the metal holds onto its electrons much tighter (e.g., Platinum vs. Sodium).
*   **Visual Overlays:**
    *   **Photons (Light beams):** Fired from the left.
    *   **Electrons (Blue dots):** Ejected from the metal plate. Notice their speed when you highly exceed the work function!
