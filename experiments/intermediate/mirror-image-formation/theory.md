# Mirror Image Formation

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** A curved mirror (like a concave spherical mirror) focuses incoming parallel light rays to a single point called the focal point. Depending on where an object is placed relative to this focal point, the mirror creates an image that may be real or virtual, magnified or reduced, upright or inverted.
**Real-World Example:** Makeup and shaving mirrors are usually slightly concave. When you stand very close to them (inside the focal point), they provide an upright and highly magnified *virtual image* of your face. But if you back far away, the image flips upside down, becoming a *real image*!

## Formulas Used
The governing formula for spherical mirrors is the **Mirror Equation**:

$$
\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{d_o} + \frac{1}{d_i}
$$

Where:
*   $f$ is the focal length of the mirror.
*   $d_o$ is the object distance (distance from object to mirror).
*   $d_i$ is the image distance (distance from image to mirror). 

**Magnification ($M$):**
$$
M = \frac{h_i}{h_o} = -\frac{d_i}{d_o}
$$
*   If $M$ is negative, the image is inverted (upside down).
*   If $|M| > 1$, the image is magnified.

**Ray Tracing Rules:**
1.  A ray parallel to the axis reflects through the focal point ($f$).
2.  A ray through the center of curvature ($2f$) reflects straight back on itself.

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **Where and how large is the reflection from a curved mirror?** to master ray diagram construction and understand how focal length alters image projection.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Object Distance ($d_o$):** Move the object closer or further from the mirror surface. Watch what happens when it crosses the red focal point!
*   **Object Height ($h_o$):** Adjust the vertical size of the source object.
*   **Focal Length ($f$):** Changes the curvature of the mirror. A concave mirror has a positive focal length.
*   **Ray Tracing Overlay:**
    *   **Parallel Ray:** Top light beam.
    *   **Focal Ray:** Secondary tracking beam showing the exact optical intersection point.
