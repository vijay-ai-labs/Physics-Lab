# Speed = Distance / Time

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** Kinematics describes motion. The most fundamental concept of motion is linear velocity (speed in a straight line), which relates how far an object travels (distance) to the duration it takes to get there (time).
**Real-World Example:** If you drive a car on the highway at a constant speed of 100 kilometers per hour, you will cover a distance of 100 kilometers in exactly 1 hour. If you speed up, you cover the same distance in less time.

## Formulas Used
The standard formula for constant velocity (or average speed) is:

$$
v = \frac{d}{t}
$$

Where:
*   $v$ is speed or velocity (meters per second, m/s)
*   $d$ is distance traveled (meters, m)
*   $t$ is elapsed time (seconds, s)

You can rearrange this formula to solve for any of the three variables:
$$
d = v \cdot t
$$
$$
t = \frac{d}{v}
$$

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **What is the speed when distance and time are known?** to help us deeply understand the strict inverse proportional relationship between speed and time for a fixed distance.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Distance (m):** The target goal line the car must reach. 
*   **Speed (m/s):** The constant velocity of the car. Increasing this will shorten the time it takes to reach the finish line.
*   **Target Time (s):** Alternatively, you can slide this to set a specific target time you want the car to take. The simulation will automatically calculate and adjust the Speed required to hit that exact time.
