# Work Done

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** In physics, "work" is done when a force acting upon an object causes a displacement of that object. If you apply a force but the object doesn't move, zero work has been done. If you push it and it moves, work is done!
**Real-World Example:** Pushing a massive stalled car to the side of the road. No matter how hard you push (force), if the car doesn't roll forward (displacement), you've done zero mechanical work. When the car finally squeaks forward, you are doing work on it. The amount of work depends on how hard you pushed and how far it rolled.

## Formulas Used
The formula for Work ($W$) is:

$$
W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos(\theta)
$$

Where:
*   $W$ is the work done (measured in Joules, J).
*   $F$ is the magnitude of the applied force (Newtons, N).
*   $d$ is the displacement or distance moved (meters, m).
*   $\theta$ is the angle between the force vector and the direction of displacement. In this 1D simulation, the force is perfectly aligned with motion ($\theta=0 \implies \cos(0)=1$), simplifying the equation to $W = F \cdot d$.

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **How much work is done by a force over a distance?** to easily visualize the direct relationship between force, distance traveled, and the resulting work calculated line graph.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Force (N):** The constant push applied to the block along the track.
*   **Displacement limit (m):** How far the block is allowed to travel before stopping.
*   **Vector Overlays:**
    *   **Force Vector:** Visualizes the strength of the push.
    *   **Distance Arrow:** Represents the displacement scale as the object crosses the distance threshold.
