# Pendulum Motion

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** A simple pendulum consists of a mass (the bob) hanging from a string. When pulled slightly to one side and released, it acts as a harmonic oscillator—swinging back and forth due to gravity constantly pulling it back toward the lowest center point.
**Real-World Example:** Grandfather clocks use swinging pendulums to keep near-perfect time! In playgrounds, swings act as large human pendulums.

## Formulas Used
The most fascinating property of a pendulum is that its period (the time it takes to complete one full back-and-forth swing) depends *only* on the length of the string and the gravitational pull, completely disregarding the weight of the bob!

**Time Period ($T$):**
$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}
$$

Where:
*   $T$ is the Period in seconds (s).
*   $L$ is the Length of the pendulum string (meters, m).
*   $g$ is the acceleration due to gravity (9.81 m/s² on Earth).

The instantaneous frequency is $f = \frac{1}{T}$. 

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **What is the time period for a given pendulum length?** to help us prove that changing the initial angle or mass (not modelled since it cancels out) does not change the rhythm of the swing, but string length critically does.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Length (m):** The length of the string. Making this longer significantly slows down the swing (increases the Time Period).
*   **Initial Angle (°):** How far back you pull the bob before dropping it. For small angles (<15°), the period formula above is extremely accurate.
*   **Gravity (m/s²):** The strength of the planet's gravitational pull. Try lowering this to simulate the Moon, and watch the pendulum swing drastically slower!
*   **Vector Overlays:**
    *   **Velocity:** Real-time speed tangent to the curve. Peaking at the bottom!
    *   **Gravity:** Constant downward pull vector.
