# Kinetic Energy

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** Kinetic Energy (KE) is the energy of motion. Any object that has mass and is moving has kinetic energy. It tells us how much mechanical effort was required to accelerate the object from rest to its current velocity, or how much effort it would require to stop it.
**Real-World Example:** A rolling bowling ball and a fast-pitched baseball both have kinetic energy. Which one has more? A bowling ball is heavy (large mass) but a baseball is fast (large velocity). Because kinetic energy scales with the *square* of the velocity, doubling your speed quadruples your kinetic energy! This is why high-speed car crashes are much more devastating than low-speed ones.

## Formulas Used
The formula for translational Kinetic Energy is:

$$
KE = \frac{1}{2} m v^2
$$

Where:
*   $KE$ is the Kinetic Energy (Joules, J).
*   $m$ is the mass of the object (kilograms, kg).
*   $v$ is the velocity or speed of the object (meters per second, m/s).

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **How much kinetic energy does a moving object have?** to help us visualize the exponential relationship of speed vs. energy—proving why speeding is disproportionately dangerous compared to just driving a heavy vehicle.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Mass (kg):** Adjusts the weight of the ball. Increasing mass scales the KE linearly (e.g. 2x mass = 2x energy).
*   **Velocity (m/s):** Adjusts the speed of the ball. Increasing velocity scales the KE exponentially (e.g. 2x speed = 4x energy).
*   **Vector Overlays:**
    *   **Speed:** Real-time visual motion indicator comparing the simulated rate of the object.
