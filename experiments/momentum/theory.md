# Momentum Conservation

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** The Law of Conservation of Momentum states that in a closed system (no external forces like friction), the total momentum of two objects before a collision is exactly equal to their total momentum after the collision.
**Real-World Example:** Playing pool (billiards). When the fast-moving cue ball strikes a stationary colored ball, the cue ball slows down or stops, and the colored ball rockets forward. The momentum from the cue ball was transferred directly to the other ball, keeping the total momentum of the table constant.

## Formulas Used
**Momentum ($p$)** is simply mass times velocity:
$$
p = m \cdot v
$$

Total momentum before collision equals total momentum after:
$$
m_1v_{1\text{ (initial)}} + m_2v_{2\text{ (initial)}} = m_1v_{1\text{ (final)}} + m_2v_{2\text{ (final)}}
$$

**Coefficient of Restitution ($e$):** This describes how "bouncy" the collision is.
$$
e = \frac{v_{2\text{ (final)}} - v_{1\text{ (final)}}}{v_{1\text{ (initial)}} - v_{2\text{ (initial)}}}
$$
*   If $e = 1$: Perfectly elastic (billiard balls). Kinetic energy is fully conserved.
*   If $e = 0$: Perfectly inelastic (clay balls). The objects stick together and move at the same speed. Much kinetic energy is lost to heat/deformation.

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **How is momentum conserved in collisions?** to understand the predictable transfer of speed and energy between objects of different masses bouncing off each other.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Mass of Blue/Red Car (kg):** Adjusts the relative weights. A heavier blue car will easily push through a lighter red car.
*   **Collision Elasticity ($e$):** 1.0 means a perfect bounce. 0.0 means they crash and stick together like a car accident.
*   **Vector Overlays:**
    *   **Velocity:** Real-time speed of each block.
    *   **Momentum:** The combined vector representing total "impact power" ($p = mv$). Note how the sum of both momentum vectors remains identical before and after impact!
