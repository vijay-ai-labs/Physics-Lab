# Ohm's Law Circuit

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** Ohm's Law states that the electrical current flowing through a metallic conductor is strictly proportional to the voltage applied across it, provided the temperature and physical conditions remain constant. The resistance dictates how easily the electrons are allowed to flow.
**Real-World Example:** Think of electricity like water flowing through a garden hose. 
*   **Voltage** is the water pressure from the faucet pushing the water. 
*   **Current** is the actual rate the water is flowing out of the nozzle. 
*   **Resistance** is "kinking" the hose. The more you kink it (high resistance), the less water flows (low current), even if the faucet pressure is on full blast!

## Formulas Used
The foundational formula for all circuit calculations is **Ohm's Law**:

$$
V = I \cdot R
$$

You can rearrange this triangle algebraically to solve for whatever variable is currently missing:
*   To find Current when you know the battery and the resistor:
$$
I = \frac{V}{R}
$$
*   To find the required resistor to block flow:
$$
R = \frac{V}{I}
$$

Where:
*   $V$ is Voltage or Potential Difference (Volts, V).
*   $I$ is Current (Amperes, A).
*   $R$ is Resistance (Ohms, $\Omega$).

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **What is the current (I) when voltage and resistance change?** to understand the fundamental relationship driving all modern electricity and observe how resistors bottleneck an active circuit.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Voltage (V):** Adjust the strength of the battery source. Notice how dialing up the voltage increases the speed of the moving electrons.
*   **Resistance ($\Omega$):** Adjust the strength of the inline resistor. Dialing this up severely restricts the circuit, slowing the electrons to a crawl.
*   **Circuit Overlay:**
    *   **Electron Flow:** Moving dots visualizing the calculated Amperage. They move rapidly in high-current scenarios and slowly in high-resistance scenarios.
