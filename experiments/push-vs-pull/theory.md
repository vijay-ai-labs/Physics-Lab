# Push vs Pull

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** A force is a push or a pull acting upon an object as a result of its interaction with another object. Forces can cause an object to accelerate, decelerate, remain in place, or change shape.
**Real-World Example:** Think about moving a heavy box across the floor. You can stand behind it and *push* it forward, or stand in front of it and *pull* it towards you. Both actions apply a force to the box, causing it to move against the resistance of the floor (friction).

## Formulas Used
The core principle governing this motion is Newton's Second Law of Motion:

$$
F_{\text{net}} = m \cdot a
$$

Where $F_{\text{net}}$ is the total (net) force acting on the object, $m$ is the mass, and $a$ is the resulting acceleration.

If we account for the applied force ($F_{\text{app}}$) and opposing kinetic friction ($F_{\text{frict}}$):

$$
F_{\text{net}} = F_{\text{app}} - F_{\text{frict}}
$$

The force of kinetic friction is:

$$
F_{\text{frict}} = \mu_k \cdot F_{\text{normal}} = \mu_k \cdot (m \cdot g)
$$

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **How does applied force affect motion of an object?** to help us understand the relationship between applied force, mass, friction, and the resulting acceleration of an object.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Applied Force (N):** The strength of the push or pull on the block. A positive value pushes the block right, while a negative value (or pulling from the left) acts in the opposite direction.
*   **Mass (kg):** The mass of the block. A heavier block requires more force to achieve the same acceleration and generates more friction.
*   **Friction Coefficient ($\mu$):** The roughness of the surface. A higher value means more resistance to movement.
*   **Vector Overlays:**
    *   **Velocity:** Shows the speed and direction of the block's movement.
    *   **Applied Force:** Shows the direction and magnitude of your push/pull.
    *   **Friction:** Shows the opposing force generated by the surface.
    *   **Net Force:** Shows the total resulting force ($F_{\text{app}} - F_{\text{frict}}$) that causes acceleration.
