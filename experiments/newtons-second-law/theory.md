# Newton's Second Law

## What Is This Experiment?
**Definition:** Newton's Second Law defines the relationship between an object's mass, its acceleration, and the applied force. It states that the acceleration of an object depends directly on the net force acting upon it, and inversely on its mass.
**Real-World Example:** Imagine pushing a shopping cart. If the cart is empty (low mass), a small push (force) gives it a big acceleration. If the cart is full of groceries (high mass), that exact same push will result in much slower acceleration.

## Formulas Used
The foundational formula for classical mechanics is:

$$
F = m \cdot a
$$

Where:
*   $F$ is the net force applied (Newtons, N)
*   $m$ is the mass of the object (kilograms, kg)
*   $a$ is the resulting acceleration (meters per second squared, m/s²)

When friction is present, the *net* force is the difference between your push and the friction resisting it:
$$
F_{\text{net}} = F_{\text{app}} - F_{\text{frict}}
$$
$$
a = \frac{F_{\text{app}} - \mu \cdot m \cdot g}{m}
$$

## Goal of the Experiment
The main objective is to find **What acceleration does a force produce on a mass?** to help us understand how changing the mass or adding friction limits the top speed and rate of acceleration of a driven object.

## Simulation Controls
*   **Applied Force (N):** The forward push. Increasing this linearly increases acceleration.
*   **Mass (kg):** The weight of the block. Increasing this inversely decreases acceleration.
*   **Friction ($\mu$):** The roughness of the surface. If this is too high, the applied force might be less than static friction, meaning the block won't move at all!
*   **Vector Overlays:**
    *   **Velocity:** The instantaneous speed resulting from the accumulated acceleration ($v = v_0 + at$).
    *   **Acceleration:** The rate of change of speed ($a = F_{\text{net}}/m$).
    *   **Net Force:** The total effective force acting horizontally.
